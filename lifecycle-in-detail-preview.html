The Lifecycle in Detail

Review cadence: quarterly
Policy. Every AI/ML solution at [Organisation] moves through the six phases described on this page. The artefacts and gate questions noted in each section establish current expectations. As the AI/ML function matures, expectations will tighten and selected artefacts will become mandatory.

Purpose

This page is the working reference for the six lifecycle phases introduced on the AI/ML Solution Lifecycle parent page. For each phase it sets out the objective, the key activities, the artefacts produced, the department accountable, and the gate question that governs the move to the next phase. The full RACI and the explicit criteria behind each gate live on the Roles, Accountabilities, and Governance Gates child page.

None of the artefacts described below are mandatory today; that will change as the function matures, with the model card and the Change Classification Record the most likely to harden first.

Intake

Intake is the front door. Candidate use cases are submitted through the AI Front Door portal and triaged by the DS community against published criteria — does the use case have a clear sponsor, a stated business problem, and a plausible hypothesis that data can help. Use cases that do not meet the criteria are declined with a reason recorded; those that do are accepted, with a working accountable from DS assigned to carry them forward.

The detailed criteria for the AI Front Door — submission format, triage cadence, and grounds for rejection — live on the Intake / AI Front Door child page, which the DS community owns.

Feasibility

Feasibility is a deliberately lightweight, cross-team viability check. Its purpose is to filter, not to design. The business outcome is restated in measurable terms, and the use case is tested for viability from several angles at once: whether the data can be sourced and pipelined (Data Solution and Data Engineering), whether it can be operated and supported once built (MLOps, with Platform on resources and environments), and whether it is permissible to address this problem with AI at all (AI Governance). DS coordinates the check and owns the recommendation, but the judgement is a shared one.

Anything that turns into substantial data work or modelling has crossed into Discovery and should be re-scoped accordingly. The phase closes with a go/no-go recommendation captured in a feasibility memo.

Discovery

Discovery determines, with evidence, whether the use case should be productionised. The team explores the data, frames the problem and selects a method — including the honest question of whether AI/ML is the right tool or whether something simpler meets the outcome — builds a proof of concept, and pilots it in the Discovery environment, a production-equivalent environment that allows realistic testing without affecting live systems.

The pilot sits at the end of Discovery rather than the start of Productionise. This is deliberate: the pilot exists to answer “should we productionise this?”, not to ship to users. The phase produces a pilot evaluation report and a draft model card alongside the POC.

Productionise

Productionise is the longest and most engineering-heavy phase. The solution is built to production quality, validated against the success criteria agreed in Discovery, deployed, and scaled to its intended users, with monitoring, alerting, and on-call coverage established. It is also the phase most prone to going wrong if treated as a continuation of Discovery — Discovery code rarely survives Productionise unchanged.

The handover from DS to MLOps sits inside this phase as an internal gate, not a separate phase. Accountability transfers from DS to MLOps once the handover criteria are met, between deployment-at-scale and the move to Monitor — a deliberate, evidenced transition rather than a silent transfer.

Monitor

Monitor is steady-state operation, owned end-to-end by MLOps. It covers drift and performance monitoring against agreed metrics, incident response, and scheduled retraining on refreshed data where the architecture and features are unchanged. Every proposed change to the live solution is classified against the Material Change Rubric (below) before any work begins.

Changes that clear the rubric as in-flight stay in Monitor and are handled by MLOps. Changes flagged as material loop the solution back to Discovery, at which point DS re-engages and the solution re-enters the gates from there.

Retirement

Retirement decommissions a solution cleanly, preserves the institutional memory of what it did and why, and frees up the capacity it consumed. A solution enters Retirement when any trigger holds: the outcome is no longer relevant, a replacement is going live, performance has degraded beyond recovery, a regulatory change makes it non-viable, or the cost-to-value balance has tipped and cannot be recovered.

The work itself — notifying consumers, switching off serving, decommissioning infrastructure, archiving artefacts and decisions, and capturing lessons learned — is recorded in a retirement record. Retirement is the only phase whose exit has no next phase; its completion closes the lifecycle for that solution.

Aspiration. Retirement is described here as target-state. As a new function, we have not yet retired a live AI/ML solution; the detail will firm up the first time it is exercised.

The Material Change Rubric

Every proposed change to a live solution is classified against the Material Change Rubric before any work begins. The rubric makes a single judgement explicit: should this change be handled inside Monitor, or does it loop the solution back to Discovery? The change is tested against four questions; if the answer to any one is yes, the change is material and the solution returns to Discovery (or to Feasibility, if the underlying use case itself is changing). If all four are no, the change is in-flight and stays in Monitor.

Worked examples

  • Retraining the existing model on the latest three months of data, same features and architecture. In-flight — nothing changes about what the model does, needs, who it affects, or its risk tier.
  • Swapping the underlying embedding model in a RAG solution. Material — predictions for the same input will change.
  • Adding a new feature derived from existing data to improve accuracy. Material — the model now needs something it did not before, and what it does has changed.
  • Extending an internal-only solution to a new business unit. Material — who it affects has changed.
  • Tightening an alerting threshold on the monitoring dashboard. In-flight — no change to the model itself.
Guidance. When in doubt about a classification, treat the change as material. The cost of an unnecessary Discovery loop is lower than the cost of an undetected material change reaching production.
